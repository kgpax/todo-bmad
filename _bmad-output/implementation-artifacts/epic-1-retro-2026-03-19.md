- Previous retrospective available: No

## Epic Summary

Epic 1 finished with 10 completed stories, but it did not begin that way. The original epic plan had 9 stories, and Story 1.10 was added during implementation after the team raised the quality bar around Definition of Done enforcement and test confidence. That change improved the epic rather than destabilizing it: the added story converted evolving quality expectations into explicit coverage enforcement and stronger verification rules. Across those 10 completed stories, the epic established the project foundation across the monorepo, shared API contract, backend CRUD endpoints, ISR page rendering, todo creation flow, E2E coverage, and enforced 100% unit/integration coverage thresholds. The epic delivered the first two user journeys and proved that the early structural decisions were usable in real implementation, not just valid on paper.

## What Went Well

- Early foundational stories paid off. Later stories were able to build on the shared contract, backend patterns, and frontend structure instead of re-solving those problems.
- Separation of concerns was strong. Frontend, backend, and shared responsibilities remained clear throughout the epic.
- The architecture held up under implementation pressure. The frontend, backend, and shared package boundaries remained useful as complexity increased.
- Code review added real value. Multiple stories improved after review through accessibility, contrast, hydration-safety, test-coverage, cleanup, and documentation fixes.
- Quality maturity increased across the epic. By the end of Epic 1, the team had unit/integration coverage thresholds at 100%, isolated E2E journeys, Page Object Models, and Lighthouse verification in place.
- The team surfaced subtle issues before they became recurring defects, including hydration mismatch risks, stale timestamp rendering under ISR, E2E test isolation problems, and cleanup concerns in async UI code.

## Challenges And Friction

- The Definition of Done was not solid enough at the start of Epic 1. Required verification steps were discovered and strengthened incrementally instead of being enforced from story one.
- Even after the DoD became clearer, enforcement at story completion was inconsistent. Some stories were treated as effectively done before every required gate had been explicitly checked.
- Material deviations were sometimes absorbed inside active stories instead of being elevated as their own tracked follow-up or planning change. This reduced traceability.
- Several quality issues were caught reactively in review instead of being prevented earlier by a stronger completion checklist.
- Some implementation details revealed planning assumptions that need tighter validation before Epic 2, especially around completed-item ordering requirements.

## Patterns Observed From Story Records

- Story 1.8 surfaced a hydration mismatch caused by server/client randomness and fixed it by selecting the empty-state message server-side.
- Story 1.7 required accessibility contrast corrections during review, showing that visual quality gates were useful but not yet preventative.
- Story 1.9 surfaced multiple process and implementation lessons: timestamp handling under ISR, E2E test isolation, Page Object Model adoption, and cleanup for transient UI timers.
- Story 1.10 was added after Epic 1 had already begun. Its addition is evidence that the team strengthened the quality model during execution rather than leaving known verification gaps unresolved.
- Story 1.10 converted quality expectations into an enforced system by adding coverage thresholds and updating the project Definition of Done.

## Key Insights

1. Foundational stories did their job: later delivery proved the value of the early groundwork.
2. Process quality improved during the epic, but too much of that improvement was reactive.
3. "Done" must mean verified, not just implemented.
4. One canonical Definition of Done is useful, but agents need the checklist surfaced at the exact point where they declare work complete.
5. Simpler universal rules are better than conditional quality gates that rely on judgment.

## Action Items

### Process Improvements

1. Add a mandatory DoD verification checklist to every implementation handoff.
   - Owner: Bob (Scrum Master)
   - Timing: Before the next story is marked done, ready for review, or ready to commit
   - Success criteria: Every story closeout explicitly confirms lint, build, unit/integration tests with coverage gate, E2E, and Lighthouse status

2. Standardize story status language.
   - Owner: Bob (Scrum Master)
   - Timing: Before Epic 2 implementation begins
   - Success criteria: If code is implemented but verification is incomplete, the story is labeled "implementation complete, verification incomplete" rather than done

3. Track material deviations as explicit follow-up items instead of burying them inside story scope.
   - Owner: Alice (Product Owner) and Bob (Scrum Master)
   - Timing: Starting with the next material scope change
   - Success criteria: Any behavior, architecture, or DoD-affecting deviation is logged as a distinct planning or follow-up item

### Quality And Verification

4. Make Lighthouse mandatory for every implementation story.
   - Owner: Dana (QA Engineer)
   - Timing: Effective for Epic 2 onward
   - Success criteria: Lighthouse is run regardless of whether the story appears frontend- or backend-focused

5. Surface the canonical DoD at the point of completion, not only in project guidance.
   - Owner: Bob (Scrum Master) and Dana (QA Engineer)
   - Timing: Before the next implementation handoff
   - Success criteria: Agents reference the same canonical DoD and repeat it in the final completion checklist

### Epic 2 Preparation

6. Review the completed-item ordering requirement before or at the start of Story 2.1.
   - Owner: Winston (Architect) and Alice (Product Owner)

- Universal verification rules are preferred over conditional verification rules.
- If a change affects downstream assumptions, it must be made visible as a planning artifact, not left as a buried note.

## Closing Summary

Epic 1 was a successful first epic. It delivered the intended user-facing foundation and, just as importantly, exposed where the team's process needed to mature. The epic should not be described as a static execution of an unchanged plan: it began with 9 stories and improved itself by adding Story 1.10 once stronger DoD enforcement and higher test confidence became necessary. The main lesson is not that the team lacked standards; it is that the enforcement mechanism for those standards must be tighter and more visible. If the action items from this retrospective are applied before Epic 2, the team should be able to preserve Epic 1's strong architectural momentum while reducing avoidable verification drift.
